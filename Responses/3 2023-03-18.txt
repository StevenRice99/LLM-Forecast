 Article 9 
 The article discusses lessons learned from the COVID-19 pandemic in Alberta, Canada, and how they can be applied to future public health crises.
 Key takeaways include:
 + Science evolves: Scientific knowledge can change rapidly, and public health officials must be prepared to adapt their recommendations accordingly.
 + Trust is a two-way street: The government and public health officials must communicate clearly and transparently with the public, while also acknowledging and addressing concerns and mistrust.
 + Socio-economic divisions were amplified: The pandemic highlighted existing socio-economic divisions in society, with more advantaged people being more willing to trust Canadian institutions.
 + Vaccine uptake was high: Despite some resistance, a significant majority of Albertans (78%) received their initial COVID-19 shots.
 Challenges that may arise if another pandemic occurs include:
 + Distrust and polarization: The experience of the past three years has created distrust among some segments of society, which could make it more difficult to implement public health measures in the future.
 + Lessons not learned: Some experts worry that the lessons from the pandemic will not be applied, leading to a repeat of mistakes made during COVID-19.
