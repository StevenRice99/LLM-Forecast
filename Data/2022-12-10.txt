Title: Children as an afterthought during COVID-19: defining a child-inclusive ethical framework for pandemic policymaking - BMC Medical Ethics
Publisher: BMC Medical Ethics
Posted: 5 days ago
The article discusses the importance of considering social and mental health impacts alongside biological impacts when planning public health measures for pandemic management. The authors propose an expanded framework for protecting young people from harm, which includes not only physical health but also educational, community, and parental resources.
Key points:
The COVID-19 pandemic has highlighted the need to consider social and mental health impacts in addition to biological impacts.
Young people have been disproportionately affected by lockdowns, with challenges in accessing mental health services and community programs.
An expanded framework for protecting young people from harm should include a broader definition of harm that encompasses social and mental health impacts.
This framework should prioritize mitigation strategies beyond the healthcare field and consider resource investments as an expanded "duty to provide care".
The article acknowledges challenges and limitations, including the need for future work to engage in retrospective reflection on lessons and advances discussed in this paper.
The authors emphasize the importance of considering young people's experiences and perspectives when planning public health measures. They also highlight the need for accountability, inclusiveness, transparency, and reasonableness in responding to social impacts.
Key takeaways:
Consideration of social and mental health impacts is essential when planning public health measures.
An expanded framework for protecting young people from harm should prioritize mitigation strategies beyond the healthcare field.
Resource investments should be considered as an expanded "duty to provide care".
Young people's experiences and perspectives should be prioritized in planning public health measures.
Accountability, inclusiveness, transparency, and reasonableness are essential when responding to social impacts.
