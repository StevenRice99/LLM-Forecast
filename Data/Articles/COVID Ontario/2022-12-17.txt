Title: 2 Montreal children have died from group A strep complications. Here's what parents need to know
Publisher: CBC.ca
Trusted: False
Posted: Today
Here are the key points from the article about Group A Streptococcus (iGAS) infections:
What is iGAS? 
iGAS stands for Invasive Group A Streptococcal disease, a type of bacterial infection caused by Streptococcus pyogenes.
Serious illnesses caused by iGAS: 
1. Strep throat
2. Meningitis
3. Septic arthritis (flesh-eating disease)
4. Necrotizing fasciitis
5. Strep toxic shock syndrome
Who is most at risk? 
Children under 1 year old, those who haven't been vaccinated against flu or chickenpox, immunocompromised people, and those with skin conditions like eczema.
Symptoms to watch out for in kids: 
Trouble breathing
Fever
Change in personality
Fatigue
Stiff neck
Full-body rash
Unable to eat or drink
No longer urinating
What about adults? 
Signs of a possible serious infection include high fever, difficulty breathing, fatigue and confusion. Widespread reddening of the skin is also another symptom.
How can people protect themselves and children? 
1. Proper hand washing before eating and after coughing or sneezing
2. Watching for skin infections
3. Coughing or sneezing into a tissue or elbow
4. Disinfecting frequently touched surfaces and objects
5. Visiting a healthcare provider if a child has suspected strep throat
Is iGAS circulating in Canada? 
Yes, it is circulating in Canada every year, with an average of 2,000 to 3,000 cases annually.
Why are we seeing more iGAS infections now? 
The article suggests that the current viral illnesses (e.g. influenza and chickenpox) may be contributing to a higher risk of iGAS infections.
